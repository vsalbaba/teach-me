1. What's Actually Happening

You described the problem perfectly: the tip stops "attracting" solder, and it beads up and rolls off. In Lesson 1, you learned that oxide blocks wetting on PCB pads. The exact same thing happens to your tip.

Your tip is a heat-transfer tool. Its only job is to conduct heat from the heating element to the joint. When the tip is coated in a thin layer of solder (tinned), it transfers heat efficiently -- the molten solder on the tip makes direct metal-to-metal contact with the workpiece. When the tip is oxidized, the oxide layer acts as a thermal insulator. Heat can't get through. Solder can't wet. Everything fails.

Source: Hakko -- Keeping the Tip in Good Condition

This is why an oxidized tip feels like it "stopped heating up" even though the temperature readout hasn't changed. The element is hot, but the oxide is blocking the heat from reaching the solder.

2. What's Inside Your Tip

Understanding tip construction explains why certain things kill tips and others don't.

Cross-section of a soldering tip: ┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ Iron plating (working │ <-- Thin protective layer │ surface you solder with) │ Wets with solder │ ┌───────────────────────┐ │ Oxidizes over time │ │ │ │ │ │ Copper core │ │ <-- Excellent heat conductor │ │ (bulk of the tip) │ │ Dissolves into solder │ │ │ │ if exposed │ └───────────────────────┘ │ └─────────────────────────────┘

The copper core conducts heat from the heating element to the tip surface. The iron plating protects the copper from dissolving into the solder (copper is highly soluble in molten tin). The iron layer is thin -- typically 30-50 microns.

The iron plating is the tip's lifespan. Once the iron is gone (from wear, corrosion, or physical damage), the copper underneath dissolves into solder, creating pits and holes. No amount of tinning or flux can fix a tip with compromised plating.

Source: iFixit -- How to Clean and Tin a Tip, Metcal -- How to Remove Oxidation

3. How Oxidation Kills Your Tip

Oxidation is a chemical reaction: iron + oxygen = iron oxide. It happens at room temperature (slowly) and at soldering temperatures (fast). Here's how it progresses:

Healthy
Tip is tinned -- a thin solder coat covers the iron. Solder wets easily. Heat transfers efficiently. The solder layer seals the iron from air.
Exposed
Solder coat burns off or is wiped away. The bare iron is now exposed to air at 300+C. Oxide starts forming immediately -- you have seconds, not minutes.
Oxidizing
Oxide layer grows. The tip darkens. Solder starts to bead instead of coating. You can still fix this easily with flux and fresh solder.
Severely oxidized
Thick oxide layer. Tip is dark grey or black. Solder balls up completely and rolls off. Normal tinning doesn't work -- the oxide is too thick for rosin-core flux to cut through. You need chemical tip tinner.
Damaged
Iron plating compromised. Pits, holes, or cracks in the plating. Copper is dissolving. The tip is dying. Replace it.

Sources: Metcal, Hakko

Temperature is the accelerator. Oxidation at 400C happens roughly twice as fast as at 350C. Your old transformer iron had no temperature control -- it may have been running at 450C+ and cooking the tips into oblivion while you worked. The Pinecil V2's temperature control is your biggest weapon against this.

Source: Hakko -- How to Maximize Tip Life

4. The Four Habits That Prevent It

Tip care is not a repair procedure. It's a set of habits you build into every soldering session. Think of it as a ritual with four moments:

Start of session
Heat up, then clean, then tin. Power on the Pinecil. Wait for it to reach temperature. Wipe the old solder off on brass wool. Immediately melt fresh solder onto the tip. The tip should look shiny silver. Now you're ready to solder.
During work
Clean before each joint, not after. This is the habit most people get backwards. Clean the tip on brass wool, then immediately make the joint. Don't clean and then set the iron down -- that leaves a bare tip exposed to air. If you're pausing between joints, leave the solder on the tip.

Source: Hakko -- "Clean the tip before soldering. If you clean it after, tip oxidation will accelerate."
Pausing (in holder)
Leave solder on the tip. When the iron sits in the holder between joints, the solder coat protects the iron from air. If it looks like the solder has burned off, add more before you set it down. If you're pausing for more than 5 minutes, turn the iron off entirely -- the Pinecil V2 also has a sleep mode that drops temperature automatically.

Source: Hakko, PINE64 Wiki
End of session
Tin generously, then power off. Before you turn off the iron, melt a generous blob of solder onto the entire working surface of the tip -- not just the very point, but the sides too. This blob will solidify and seal the tip from air while it's stored. Next time you heat up, you'll wipe this off and re-tin with fresh solder.

Source: iFixit, Stellar Technical
Check your brass wool situation. Do you have a brass wool tip cleaner? If not, this is the one thing worth buying. It cleans without dropping the tip temperature (unlike a wet sponge) and leaves a thin solder film behind. A brass wool cleaner with a rosin-filled base (like the Hakko 599B style) is ideal -- the rosin adds a bit of flux during cleaning.

If you've been using a damp sponge, you can keep using it, but know that every wipe drops the tip temperature by 10-20C and removes all solder -- leaving the tip temporarily bare and vulnerable. Brass wool is gentler.

5. Rescuing an Oxidized Tip

If your tip is already dark and refusing solder, here's the escalation ladder. Start at Level 1 and move up only if the previous step doesn't work.

Flux + fresh solder

For light oxidation (tip is dull but not black). Set the Pinecil to ~300C. Apply your NC-223-ASM flux paste directly to the tip. Immediately melt solder onto the fluxed surface. The flux dissolves the thin oxide, and the solder wets and tins the tip. Wipe on brass wool. Repeat if needed.

This is the same fix from Lesson 1, Quiz Question 5 -- and it works for most cases.

Source: Metcal

Tip tinner (chemical paste)

For moderate-to-severe oxidation (tip is dark grey/black, flux alone doesn't cut through). Tip tinner is a small tin of aggressive flux mixed with solder powder. Heat the iron to working temperature. Press the tip into the tin and rotate slowly for 3-4 seconds. The tip should come out looking shiny. Immediately wipe off residue on brass wool, then apply fresh solder.

Use sparingly -- the aggressive flux in tip tinner eats into the iron plating over time. iFixit recommends no more than every 20-30 sessions. Think of it as medicine, not daily vitamins.

Source: iFixit, Q Source

Brass brush + tip tinner

For very stubborn oxidation. Gently brush the hot tip with a brass wire brush to mechanically break up the thickest oxide, then immediately apply tip tinner as above. The brush loosens what the chemistry can't reach.

Do not press hard. Do not use steel brushes. You're trying to dislodge oxide, not scrape the plating.

Source: iFixit

Replace the tip

If none of the above works, or you can see pits, holes, or copper-colored spots on the tip, the iron plating is compromised. No amount of flux or tip tinner will fix structural damage. Pinecil tips are relatively cheap -- buy a new one.

Source: PINE64 Wiki -- Pinecil Tips

6. What to NEVER Do

  • Never use sandpaper, files, or abrasive pads on the tip. These strip the thin iron plating, exposing the copper underneath. Once the copper is exposed, the tip degrades rapidly and permanently. (Metcal)
  • Never bang the tip against the bench to shake off solder. This can crack the iron plating. (Hakko)
  • Never leave the iron on and idle at working temperature for extended periods. The exposed tip oxidizes rapidly at 300+C with nothing to do. Use the Pinecil's sleep mode, or turn it off.
  • Never clean the tip and then put it down. Always tin immediately after cleaning. A bare, hot tip starts oxidizing within seconds.
  • Never leave the tip clean for storage. Always coat it with solder before powering down.

7. When to Give Up and Replace

Inspect your tip (while cool) with good light. Look for:

Replace if you see

  • Pits or holes in the surface
  • Copper color showing through (reddish/pinkish spots)
  • Cracks in the plating
  • Deformed or misshapen tip end
  • Solder still won't wet after tip tinner treatment

Salvageable if you see

  • Dark discoloration (oxidation, not damage)
  • Black residue on the shaft (normal flux charring)
  • Surface looks uniform but won't tin
  • No physical damage, just chemical buildup

Pinecil tips are widely available and cost a few dollars each. The PINE64 Wiki has a compatibility guide. Don't fight a dead tip -- replace it and protect the new one.

8. Pinecil V2 Specifics

Sleep mode is your friend

The Pinecil V2 has motion-based sleep. If you set it down and don't move it for a configurable period, it drops to a lower temperature automatically. This dramatically slows oxidation during pauses. Make sure this feature is enabled in your firmware settings.

New tip setup

When you get a new Pinecil tip, PINE64 recommends wiping the contacts (the white end) with isopropyl alcohol or a dry cloth to remove factory residue. Then heat it to 350C, apply solder to the working end, and wipe clean on brass wool. Repeat a few times. Some tips ship pre-tinned -- they may look odd until you wipe them clean and re-tin.

Tip swapping

Always unplug the Pinecil before changing tips. The V2 auto-detects tip resistance at power-up, so hot-swapping can cause strange temperature behavior.

Short tips need more power

If you're using Pine64's newer Short tips (ST-B2), they need more power due to lower resistance. Make sure you're using at least a 65W USB-C PD charger at 20V.

Source: PINE64 Wiki -- Pinecil Tips, PINE64 Wiki -- Guides to Soldering

Check Your Understanding

1. You just finished soldering a row of header pins. What do you do with the tip before putting the iron back in the holder?
Correct. Always leave a solder coat on the tip when it's idle. The solder seals the iron from air and prevents oxidation. A clean, bare tip starts oxidizing immediately at working temperature.
A clean, bare tip at working temperature starts oxidizing within seconds. Coat the tip with solder before setting the iron down -- the solder acts as a barrier against air.
2. Your tip is dark grey and solder beads off it. You try applying NC-223-ASM flux paste and melting solder onto it, but it still won't wet. What's the next step?
Correct. Tip tinner contains aggressive flux strong enough to cut through heavy oxidation. Use it sparingly -- the aggressive chemicals degrade the iron plating with overuse. Save sandpaper as an absolute last resort (and plan to replace the tip after).
When normal flux can't cut through, escalate to tip tinner (chemical paste). Never reach for sandpaper early -- it strips the iron plating. And cranking up heat makes oxidation worse, not better.
3. Why is brass wool preferred over a damp sponge for cleaning the tip?
Correct. A damp sponge creates a thermal shock -- the tip temperature drops 10-20C on each wipe, stressing the plating and leaving the tip bare. Brass wool cleans at temperature and leaves a thin protective solder residue behind.
Brass wool's advantage is thermal gentleness. It cleans at full temperature (no thermal shock) and leaves a thin solder film on the tip. A damp sponge drops the temperature on every wipe and strips the tip completely bare.
4. You see a small copper-colored pit on your tip. What should you do?
Correct. Exposed copper means the iron plating has been breached. The copper will dissolve into solder, enlarging the pit. No chemical treatment can rebuild the plating. The tip needs to be replaced.
Copper showing through means the iron plating is permanently compromised. No flux, tip tinner, or sanding can rebuild the plating -- the copper will continue dissolving into solder. Replace the tip.
5. When during your soldering session should you clean the tip?
Correct. Clean before the joint, then solder immediately. If you clean after, the bare tip sits exposed in the holder, oxidizing while you set up the next joint. Hakko specifically recommends this sequence.
Hakko's guidance is clear: clean before you solder, not after. Cleaning after leaves the tip bare in the holder, where it oxidizes while waiting. Clean, then solder immediately -- the joint protects the tip.

The One Rule

Everything in this lesson comes down to one idea: never leave a hot tip bare. Bare iron + oxygen + heat = oxide. Oxide blocks wetting. Blocked wetting = solder beads off.

If you build the four habits (tin at start, clean before joints, leave solder on when idle, tin before storage), your tips will last far longer than your old ones did.
